## Design Was Right. You Just Didn't Know Why.

For years, design was told to fight for a seat at the table.

And when we finally got there, the table was already covered in spreadsheets, roadmaps, and AI hype decks run by people who confuse motion with progress.

So design adapted.

We doubled down on craft. On taste. On the parts of the job that *felt human* even when the org didn't reward them.

And for a long time, that looked like failure.

Here's the part everyone missed: **design wasn't wrong — it was early.**

Now the industry is sprinting headfirst into AI like it's a life raft, not realizing it's just chaos with a pitch deck. PMs and engineers are discovering, in real time, that pattern-matching machines can replace *execution*, but not *judgment*. Not meaning. Not emotional resonance. Not trust.

AI can generate features.
It cannot decide which ones matter.

AI can summarize users.
It cannot feel them.

AI can optimize funnels.
It cannot create desire.

That gap — the uncomfortable, human gap — is where design has always lived.

And while companies are busy doing **design theater** and calling facilitation "strategy," they're quietly turning themselves into feature factories: high output, low soul, zero loyalty. Products nobody loves. Systems nobody trusts. Brands that feel like vending machines.

This is where the Patagonia-vest crowd starts panicking.

The spreadsheet priests. The KPI sommeliers. The ones who mistake control for clarity and think another dashboard will save them.

It won't.

Because the future doesn't belong to whoever adopts AI the fastest.

It belongs to whoever can **translate AI into something humans actually give a shit about**.

That's design.

Not surface-level polish. Not vibes.

**Taste as judgment. Craft as meaning. Design as sense-making in a chaotic system.**

I'm Chad. I'm a product designer *and* an engineer. I use AI daily. I embrace it fully — not because it's hype, but because it's already rewritten how we work, think, and communicate. This is the next shift of the internet age, whether skeptics like it or not.

And here's the part that should really piss people off:

Design didn't lose relevance.

**Everyone else just optimized themselves into replaceability.**

The companies that survive won't be the loudest AI adopters.

They'll be the ones who still understand humans — and put designers back where they belong:

Not at the table.

**But shaping the room.**
