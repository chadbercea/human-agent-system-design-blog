A very specific story is making the rounds right now. Designers are leaving tech. Not getting laid off. *Leaving*. On principle. Because the industry "lost its conscience." Because AI is being "forced" on them. Because the hiring market is "fucking insane."

It's a good story. Sympathetic protagonist. Clear villain. Noble exit.

It's also bullshit.

---

## The Pattern

Here's the post you've read a hundred times:

*Trust is broken. AI doesn't solve real problems. Ageism is rampant. Burnout is killing us. The hiring market is rigged. So I'm going fractional to reclaim my life.*

Every single bullet point is an external blame vector. Not one moment of: **maybe what I was doing wasn't as valuable as I thought it was, and the market is now reflecting that.**

There's no data for an "exodus." There IS data for layoffs, role consolidation, and a repricing of execution-heavy positions across the industry. Those are not the same thing. Framing a market correction as a moral departure is the first and biggest logical error in this entire narrative.

---

## The Two Assumptions Nobody Wants to Examine

### Assumption 1: "The industry used to have a conscience."

This is the big one. The entire thing is built on a before-and-after. There WAS a time when tech companies valued people, shared designers' ethics, respected craft, cared about the user. And now they don't.

That time never existed.

There is no ethical consumption under capitalism. This is not a radical Marxist take. This is an observable, repeatedly demonstrated structural reality. The phone your user-centered app runs on was assembled by someone whose working conditions would make your "burnout" look like a wellness retreat.

So what did designers actually have? Not ethical alignment with their employers. They had **insulation**. They sat in the comfortable middle of a value chain where the exploitation happened far enough upstream and downstream that they never had to see it.

What shifted is **who the losers are**. The window moved. The squeeze that warehouse workers, gig laborers, and offshore contractors have felt for years is now being felt by people with "Senior UX Designer" in their LinkedIn headline. The system didn't change. Your position in it did.

### Assumption 2: Slapping "UX" on the work makes it inherently valuable.

Slapping "UX" on the work didn't make it more valuable. It made it *legible to a specific era of tech culture* that was willing to pay for process-heavy, artifact-rich design cycles because execution was expensive and slow.

Now you can go from intent to working code in an afternoon. The pipeline collapses. And the people who built their identity around operating that pipeline are mistaking the collapse of the *process* for the collapse of *design itself*.

Real design — the judgment and taste and problem framing and meaning — is more valuable than ever. But "UX" as a job title was never a guarantee you were doing any of that. For a lot of people, "UX" meant you were a cog in an artifact production machine that justified its own existence through volume and ceremony.

The market is devaluing the ceremony. Not design.

---

## The Logical Breakdown

**"Trust is broken. Companies don't share our values."**
Companies never shared your values. They shared your invoices. The relationship was always transactional. What changed is that the transaction is being renegotiated because execution costs dropped.

**"AI is being forced on us and it doesn't solve real human problems."**
Five years ago: "AI can't do ideation." Three years ago: "OK it can ideate but it can't synthesize." Now: "Fine, it can synthesize, but it doesn't solve REAL human problems." The line keeps retreating and nobody stops to ask why every previous "AI can't do this" claim turned out to be wrong.

**"Ageism is pushing experienced designers out."**
Uncomfortable reframe: is it ageism, or is it that experience in a *previous paradigm* has diminishing returns in the current one? Thirty years of mastery as a carriage builder didn't make you a victim of discrimination when the automobile arrived.

**"Going fractional to reclaim my life."**
"Fractional" is the word people use when the market won't hire them full-time at their previous rate and they need to frame that as a lifestyle choice rather than a market outcome.

---

## The Carriage Builder

Imagine a master carriage builder in 1908. Twenty-five years perfecting suspension systems, wheel assemblies, interior comfort. Then the Model T rolls off the line.

The carriage builder isn't wrong that they have deep expertise. They aren't wrong that early automobiles were rough and imperfect. They aren't wrong that the transition is painful.

**They're wrong that the pain means the transition is illegitimate.**

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

The system that gassed you up doesn't need you the way you've been showing up anymore. That's it. That's the whole thing.

The "exodus" narrative serves a psychological function. It lets people preserve their professional identity while the market restructures that identity out from under them. It lets you be the protagonist walking away on principle rather than the subject of an economic correction.

The real exodus isn't designers leaving tech. It's designers leaving *the version of themselves that was defined by a process that no longer exists*.

The industry isn't losing its conscience. It's losing its overhead.

And if you can't tell the difference, that IS the problem.
