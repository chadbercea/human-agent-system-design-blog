Over the last year, we've gotten much better at putting "AI" in our product surfaces, our roadmaps, and our job titles.

I'm less convinced we've gotten equally good at building AI fluency in the people designing those experiences.

---

## Title ≠ Fluency

Putting "AI" in someone's title doesn't make them AI-native. It makes them a strong designer, who now sits on an AI team, working in a medium they may or may not actually be fluent in.

A lot does transfer: UX fundamentals, interaction design, visual systems, research craft. But one thing does not automatically transfer: treating AI as a material you design *with* and *within*, rather than as a tool you occasionally bolt on or struggle against.

I'm increasingly seeing a gap between title-level association with AI, and practice-level fluency in AI. And that gap is easy to miss, because the work still looks professional. Specs are well formatted. Figma files are polished. Experiences ship.

But if you zoom in on how some people actually experience AI in their own work, a different story shows up: AI is framed as a source of cognitive burden rather than a medium that can be shaped. Very normal knowledge-work problems get blamed on "AI" instead of on missing workflows, documentation, or discipline. Pain that's actually designable gets treated as inherent.

When that's your personal relationship to AI, it quietly shapes what you design. You add guardrails where fluency would add power. You design for a user who is as confused and overloaded as you are. The output still looks "AI-ish." But it's not AI-native.

---

## This Shows Up in Product Decisions

This isn't abstract. It leaks directly into what we ship.

If the people designing an AI product experience "memory across sessions" as an unavoidable cognitive tax, they're less likely to ask: "How might the system aggressively maintain and surface context so users don't have to?" Instead, they'll add affordances to re-ingest the same information, write guidance about "how to manage your cognitive load," or accept context loss as "just how AI work feels."

The gap between "this is hard" and "this is designable" is the gap between a mediocre AI feature and a great AI product.

When designers experience AI primarily as chat windows that exhaust them, tools that make more work for them, or mysterious systems that refactor things "behind their back," they're not well positioned to design against those issues for customers. They're living the problem, not shaping the solution.

---

## This Is Not About Competence. It's About Fluency.

I'm not saying these designers are bad at their jobs or shouldn't be on AI projects. Many are excellent UX designers who are early in their AI fluency journey. We shouldn't confuse craft excellence in UX with medium fluency in AI.

AI-native fluency comes from deep, sustained, hands-on use of AI in your own work — building and breaking workflows, treating AI as a collaborator and substrate, not just a feature. It does not come primarily from reading thought pieces about "cognitive debt," attending one-off trainings, or sprinkling AI into an existing flow and calling it done.

---

## The Real Question

Do we have any mechanism to distinguish between designers with AI in their title and designers with genuine AI fluency in their practice?

Because if we don't, then today: product decisions about AI experiences are being shaped by people whose main reference point is their own friction with AI. That friction gets laundered into frameworks, mental models, and team narratives. Those narratives then drive what we prioritize, what we consider "hard but inherent," and where we stop short of re-imagining the experience.

Customers eventually feel that as timid features, cautious UX that underestimates them, or AI that feels like a bolt-on assistant, not a deeply integrated capability.

---

## A Healthier Pattern

Acknowledge the gap openly. It's okay that not everyone is AI-native yet. It's not okay to pretend title equals fluency.

Create deliberate paths to fluency. Rotations where designers must use AI daily in their own practice. Pairing AI-fluent practitioners with those earlier in the journey. An expectation that "AI designer" means "I personally work this way with AI" — not just "I sit on an AI team."

Make fluency visible in staffing. When assigning AI-heavy work, consider demonstrated AI practice, not just role or level.

AI is not just a new feature category. It's a new medium. If we want AI products to feel like they were built from within that medium — not just about it — we need more than AI in people's titles. We need it in their hands, in their daily workflows, and eventually, in their intuition.
