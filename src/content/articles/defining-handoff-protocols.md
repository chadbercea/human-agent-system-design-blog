## Why handoffs matter

When the agent finishes a phase of work, the human often needs to review, correct, or approve before the next phase. If that handoff is fuzzy, both sides waste time.

## A simple model

> The best handoffs are **explicit**: the agent produces a defined artifact (e.g. a summary, a list of options, or a draft), and the human responds with a defined action (approve, edit, or reject with reason). Avoid open-ended “let me know what you think” on either side.

Use the same structure every time so the human knows what to look for and the agent knows what to wait for.

## Level of detail

### Too vague

“Send it to the team when ready.” The agent does not know what “ready” means or who “the team” is.

### Just right

“When the draft is complete, post it in #review with the label ‘Needs approval’ and notify @lead.” The agent has a clear action; the human has a clear place to look.

### Over-specified

A 50-step checklist for every handoff can slow things down. Reserve high detail for high-risk handoffs.

## Takeaway

> Document one or two handoff patterns per workflow and stick to them. Iterate based on where confusion or rework actually happens.
