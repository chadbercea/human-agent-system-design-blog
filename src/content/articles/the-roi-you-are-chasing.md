Let's start with the numbers, because you love numbers.

Only about 3 gigawatts of AI compute was actually installed in the US in 2025. Somewhere between 190 and 240 gigawatts was announced. 60% of the data centers currently under construction have no answer for where the power comes from. Not "the power is delayed." No answer. The absorption rate — the only metric that actually measures whether any of this is real — points to an industry that has mastered the art of announcement while being functionally incapable of delivery.

You funded this. You celebrated it. You put it in your board decks.

And now you're wondering why the ROI isn't materializing. It isn't a mystery.

---

## You Didn't Deploy AI9. You Embalmed Your Processes With It.

Here is what is actually happening inside your organization.

You are taking tools designed for asynchronous, parallel, context-hungry agent execution and wrapping them in seat licenses. You are measuring adoption in monthly actives. You are routing them through approval chains built for SOC2 compliance in 2014. You are pointing them at your existing workflow and calling it transformation.

What you are actually deploying AI into is your org chart. And your org chart is not designed for AI. It is designed to insulate the people at the top of it.

Approval chains, role-based permissions, escalation paths, headcount planning — the entire architecture of enterprise process is built around one atom: one person, one task, one approval, one handoff, sequential, observable, controllable. That structure exists not to accelerate work but to ensure that the people who never get PIP'd remain the people who make all the decisions.

AI is an existential threat to that structure. Not because it replaces workers. Because it makes the structure itself visible as overhead.

Agents don't wait for approval. They don't have a lane. They execute in parallel across contexts simultaneously. They make the sequential approval chain look like what it always was — a control mechanism, not a work mechanism. When you route agents through that structure you don't just slow them down. You invert them.

And then — and this is where it becomes indefensible — you are laying off the people doing the actual work to protect your margins. You are using AI as the justification for the cuts. While simultaneously ensuring that the layer of the organization that generates zero output and owns all the decisions remains completely untouched.

You are cutting the people who are making AI work in order to protect the people who are making AI theater.

---

## You've Seen This Movie. You Just Forgot How It Ended.

Cast your mind back to 2012. Adobe owned design. Photoshop was the industry standard, mature, deeply integrated, enterprise-contracted, unchallenged. Designers used maybe 10% of its functionality and suffered through the other 90% because there was no alternative.

Then Sketch arrived. It could do approximately 2% of what Photoshop could do. Designers left anyway. Not because Sketch was better in any comprehensive feature sense. Because the 2% it did was the actual job.

Then Figma took that 2% and made it multiplayer. Adobe didn't lose because they ran out of features. They lost because they were building for the tool's legacy rather than the designer's actual work. By the time they bought Figma for $20 billion in a deal that subsequently got blocked by regulators, the game was already over.

The next generation of work tooling is already being built around agents as the primary actor, not the human. The people actually shipping software are already using it. Not because it has more features than what you're selling them. Because the small number of things it does are the actual job.

You are Adobe. And somewhere right now, someone is building the Figma.

---

## The Workers You Keep Trying to Replace Are the Only Ones Making This Work

Your most recent reduction in force cut the people who couldn't demonstrate clear ROI. The people who survived did so largely by actually integrating agent workflows into their daily practice — running parallel tasks, building MCP toolchains, shipping at a rate that made the headcount math work in their favor.

And then you blocked Claude on the VPN. And handed them a derivative product instead. And asked them to produce five to ten times the output to feed the same reporting cadence the organization has always demanded.

The efficiency gain your workers built for themselves got absorbed entirely into increased output demands, with zero structural change to how work gets measured, valued, or rewarded.

The workers who are making AI work are doing it despite your deployment decisions, not because of them. They are buying personal subscriptions. They are routing around IT policy. They are using the tools that actually work on their own time, on their own hardware, to do the job you're paying them to do.

You are not leading an AI transformation. You are taxing one.

---

## The Executive Class Is the Bottleneck. Full Stop.

The tools that got funded, prioritized, and deployed first were the ones that solved executive problems. Copilot in Word. AI meeting summaries. Automated status reports. The entire surface of enterprise AI deployment maps almost perfectly onto the workflow of people who stopped doing the actual work a decade ago.

The reason is structural and obvious once you see it: the people making deployment decisions are the people whose problems got solved.

This is why you cannot find the macroeconomic signal. You've built a system that generates enormous spending, enormous announcement, and anemic measurable output — and then used the anemic output as justification for more conservative deployment, which produces less output, which justifies more caution. The loop is locked. You locked it.

---

## What Actually Happens Next

The companies that survive this transition will not be the ones who bought the most GPUs or filed the most AI-related patents. They will be the ones who accepted — actually accepted, not rhetorically accepted — that the unit of work has changed.

The worker who survived your last RIF because they figured this out on their own time? They know what needs to be rebuilt. They've been trying to tell you. They're in your Slack right now, watching the crickets pile up under their messages about stripping the legacy tooling down to something an agent can actually use.

You have a choice. Listen to them, restructure around what they know, and have a chance. Or keep scheduling alignment meetings about your AI strategy while the people who understand it build the future somewhere you're not.

History is not kind to the second option. Ask Adobe.
